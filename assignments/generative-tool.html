Due April 9

In this project, we’ll explore the boundaries of what a generative tool can be when it is made on the web. Students will develop a website that generates content and form by an audience’s input based on a set of rules. This can take the form of a practical tool or an abstract online experience. Students are encouraged to test and try the tool they made, along with their friends and peers.

  • Part 1: Concept & Sketches

    Come up with three distinct ideas for a website that utilizes an input and an output. Determine what the input and output would be. For instance, when the user speaks into a microphone, will text appear? Consider who or what this tool is for. Do you want to make something that is useful, semi-useful or not useful at all? Bring references and sketches for each direction. References can be websites, artworks, music, photographs, or anything else that sparks an idea.

  • Part 2: Digital Prototype

    Choose one of the three directions and begin prototyping it in Figma (or any prototyping software that you are familiar with). Feel free to also bring in code tests or references if that helps to explore your idea.

  • Part 3: Coded Draft

    Create a coded draft of your website. If you are running into technical or code issues, this is the time to simplify the idea to make it more attainable for yourself.

  • Part 4: Refinement & Testing

    Refine your work based on feedback. Get the tech in your coded draft to work. Start thinking about style and how it relates to your audience. Test the tool with at least three different people. Consider how to further develop your project based on the testers’ feedback.

  • Part 5: Polish & Present

    Complete the project, ensuring that your website is polished and fully functional. This is the culmination of your efforts, so make sure to pay attention to every detail. Develop a presentation that walks us through your process and project.
